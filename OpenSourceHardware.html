### What is Open-Source Hardware?

Hardware refers to tangible objects, such as furniture, textiles, machines, or electronic devices. Open-source hardware involves making the knowledge to replicate and further develop these products freely accessible. This includes all relevant information like design, construction, components, and instructions, collectively referred to as documentation. This allows people worldwide to learn from the experiences and innovations of others and develop new ideas based on this knowledge.

By having open access to this knowledge, users can understand how a product works and how it is made or replicated. This creates trust and transparency regarding quality, safety, and sustainability. Various approaches can be developed and tested in co-creative processes.

The community forms the basis of the open-source hardware movement. Individuals and different groups, such as NGOs, companies, and research institutes, share common inventions and form a community of interest. Motivations vary, including progress, community, global justice, and idealistic values.

### Goals and Opportunities

The vision of open-source hardware (OSH) is to make technology accessible to all humanity, promoting the common good. OSH seeks a collective knowledge base and serves the freedom to learn. The principles of the circular economy, which are fundamental to open-source hardware, focus on sustainable resource use. This leads to the development of environmentally friendly innovations and technologies that conserve resources and minimize waste.

The open-source approach ensures repairability, availability of spare parts, and minimizes waste. Local, adaptable productions are promoted to meet community and individual needs. Transparency and accessibility of knowledge encourage collaboration on collective projects to solve societal challenges. OSH supports the longevity of technologies and their rapid, targeted scalability and reproducibility through innovative decentralized production methods. The diversity of skills promotes holistic development, and the community motivates everyone to participate and learn, leading to diverse projects where knowledge is built collectively.

People can actively participate to promote their self-realization and be creative and productive. This allows them to make a difference while bringing in personal skills and interests. The open nature of OSH encourages working together on challenges and creating innovative solutions, a significant source of individual fulfillment and collective progress.

### How is Open-Source Hardware Financed?

There are many ways for OSH business models, with resources being accessed in different ways. Increasingly, OSH projects receive funding from countries, federal governments, or the EU. Large corporations recognize the opportunities and invest in the movement and specific project developments.

However, the most important support OSH receives is from the people forming a community. They engage directly in the development process, improving and expanding it with diverse knowledge. Different areas of expertise are managed by individual competencies. The community also contributes financially. Besides donations, the product is purchased in its entirety, as a kit, or in specific individual parts. Financial support for development comes from individual motivations, with people actively participating in progress and supporting the OSH movement.

### Who Owns the Knowledge?

All documentation is copyrighted from the moment of creation, making it proprietary. Through an open-source license, it becomes freely accessible again. Commonly used licenses include CERN OHL, TAPR OHL, Creative Commons, and GNU GPL.

The choice of license determines how intellectual property is protected and shared. These enable the use, adaptation, and sharing of knowledge under certain conditions. More information about licenses for your project can be found in the chapter "Intellectual Property and Licenses."

### Where is the Line Between Consumers and Developers in OSH?

Unlike proprietary projects, where there is a clear separation between producers and consumers, OSH overcomes this boundary and allows users to become prosumers. This role means more than just consuming – prosumers can be part of the development processes. They have the option to actively participate in product design without necessarily being part of the core team. This creates a cooperative network between those who use products and those who design them.

### Why Isn't Open-Source Hardware More Widespread?

Despite its many advantages, the OSH idea is only beginning to realize its potential. Possible challenges include traditional business models, intellectual property, and market structures prioritizing commercial interests. To promote dissemination, these need to be reconsidered. Knowledge transfer and active engagement require time and resources. Awareness of OSH must also grow to support broader recognition. Particularly, open-source hardware should be more firmly anchored in educational institutions to enable future generations to access the collaborative mindset.

### How Does Collaboration and Knowledge Sharing Work in the Community?

Collaboration extends through personal interactions in real life and online platforms. Development starts with project proposals and ideas. Using version control systems like Git, changes are coordinated, often by creating forks (copies) of the project before merging them into the main project. Open exchange occurs through commenting and discussion.

In the world of open-source software, there are remarkable examples. Linux, an open-source operating system, is developed by an international community and widely used. Blender, a popular open-source 3D graphics software, also benefits from collaborative development. These projects successfully use similar principles to create high-quality and accessible software for everyone.

### How Can I Enter the OSH World?

Talk to people already connected to the community or write to an organization in the movement. Find an OSH project that interests you, get in touch, and see if you can contribute. You can start with open resources and community platforms to learn more about OSH principles. Experiment with projects, share ideas, and work with others to develop your own innovations.

### How did the "Open Source Ecology Germany e. V." Association Come About?

Moe: The open-source hardware movement gained significant momentum in the USA in the early 2000s. Marcin Jakubowski founded the "Open Source Ecology" network and the central project "Global Village Construction Set." In 2011, his widely referenced TED talk created quite a stir globally. In Germany, the response was particularly sustainable, leading to the formation of a relatively stable community under the label "Open Source Ecology Germany." Initially, the development of open-source hardware was almost exclusively based on "self-exploitation" – people invested their free time or donated money directly to individuals within the community. It quickly became clear that this was not a sustainable business model. It also became apparent that a legal entity was needed to manage collective property (such as a solar plant) and conduct PR for political demands. However, it wasn't until 2016 that the non-profit association "Open Source Ecology Germany e. V." was officially founded.

### What Function Does the Association Serve for the Community?

Moe: The association was understood from the beginning as a pure tool for the community – essentially a legal vehicle that allows us to do various things. It primarily serves as an interface to the "old world." In other words, conventional organizations find it very difficult to enter into contracts with a community. The association forms the legal bridge for this. For example, as a non-profit association with a research purpose, we can acquire and manage public funding for open-source hardware development – which has become by far our main source of income. We can carry out public relations, especially political, much more effectively. Together, we can appear under the label of a non-profit legal entity at major conferences, sit on committees and panels, and draft statements on legislative proposals of the Thuringian State Parliament. All of this is managed collectively and without any form of command chains or classical hierarchy patterns. If we did, the association would lose its connection to the community and thus its founding purpose. Ultimately, the association is like a Swiss Army knife for the community.

Over the years, it has also increasingly taken on a platform character. The association maintains a fairly decent digital and physical infrastructure for its members and the community: Nextcloud, GitLab, Hedgedoc, forum, wiki, email mailboxes, etc. on the digital side, as well as OpenEcoLabs, tools, machines, office and seminar rooms on the physical side. We offer everything a willing developer needs to turn an idea into well-documented open-source hardware or take it to the next level. In our community, we also pool a lot of expertise, from documentation guidelines to legal questions and business models. Much of this expertise is networked through the association and its infrastructure – and there are always people involved. They often become members and expand the association further – and so new tools are added to the Swiss Army knife, so to speak.

### Can you briefly introduce a project within the association?

Moe: My personal favorite project in the association is "ZAC+," a zinc-air fuel cell. The maintainer behind it is Oliver Schlüter. The problem was that Oli could store the energy from his solar plant with battery systems from day to night, but not sensibly from summer to winter. So, a long-term storage solution was needed that is relatively easy to manufacture and maintain, without toxic or extremely expensive materials. Such a system could be manufactured almost anywhere in the world and repaired independently of the manufacturer. Zinc and oxygen are an excellent combination for this purpose. Industrial solutions have never really taken off, but they were more aimed at integrated battery systems. The peculiarity of ZAC+ is that the "combustion process" (electricity generation) and the "recycling process" (electricity storage) take place in two different stationary devices. This makes the system much simpler, more robust, and cheaper. Oli started with small table setups, mostly self-financed, and the association occasionally supported him with donation funds. Later, adjacent projects (such as the OpenNanoGrid) also received funding, which of course gave the project a boost. In cooperation with universities, mathematical and chemical theses could also be written. We also had a cooperation with an engineering office for several years, which was also a supporting member of the association at that time. Without the association as a legal entity, much of this would have been difficult to impossible.

### What makes the association unique? And how does the association ensure that the voices of the community are heard?

Moe: As a non-profit association, we are not committed to a business model but to our statutes, our non-profit purpose, and thus our values. As a community-based association, we also have a very different administrative structure than conventional associations. One-sided communication and decisions, unnecessary bureaucracy, or command chains simply do not work in a community context. And without the community, the association wouldn't exist! We couldn't implement any of our projects or meaningfully serve any of our statutory purposes. All members of the association are also part of the community, and without this connection, we couldn't work. Consequently, everything developed in the association is open source. All our developments are thus independent of the association and can be further developed in another part of the world. This model alone protects the association from becoming corrupted.

"If we want to organize technologies collectively as humanity – and we must if we are to make it to the next century – it must be open-source hardware."

This would fundamentally change our economy and its ecological compatibility – towards cooperative economies. Of course, there are also certain market entry barriers with open-source hardware. However, the old question of ownership is very elegantly solved, in my opinion:

It doesn't really matter who owns the individual machine, whether it’s a state or a company – as long as everyone has the right and the means to manufacture and further develop it. ### Review of Open Source Hardware

According to DIN SPEC 3105, the review process is carried out by a CAB ("Conformity Assessment Body"). This is an independent body that checks and certifies the conformity of products, services, or processes with certain standards and requirements. In the context of open-source hardware, a CAB carries out the certification of OSH projects to ensure that the documentation meets the criteria of DIN SPEC 3105.

After the publication of DIN SPEC 3105, OSEG e.V. started setting up a CAB to gain experience with the review process and DIN SPEC. Any organization can set up a CAB as long as they store and make the review process and the reviewed documentation public. In addition to OSEG e.V., OHO e.V. has also established a CAB andhas already reviewed many projects.

We at Open Source Ecology Germany e.V. have decided to carry out the review process with the help of the community. We involve all interested parties to review the documentation, rebuild the products, and further develop the process together. On pages 28-35, we give you an insight into the latest projects that have gone through the review process.

### DIN SPEC 3105

DIN SPEC 3105 defines what is needed for the documentation of open-source hardware to be called "open source." Unlike software, the documentation of hardware can look very different depending on the project. It depends on the technologies (mechanical, electronic, etc.) used in the project and the tools used to create the parts and plans.

DIN SPEC 3105 was developed between October 2018 and July 2020. In addition to OSEG e.V. and the German Institute for Standardization (DIN), over 20 other parties, such as community organizations, companies, and universities, were involved in its creation.

It is subject to ongoing testing and further development. The goal of DIN SPEC 3105 is to create clarity in the field of open-source hardware. It defines terms, requirements, and processes. This should prevent misunderstandings and promote the quality and interoperability of OSH projects. The specification helps create a common basis for developers, manufacturers, and users, making communication, cooperation, and knowledge exchange easier. It contributes to the further development of the OSH movement by establishing standards for openness, quality, and collaboration.

DIN SPEC 3105 is still very general but provides some key points that must be included in open-source hardware documentation. More information on this can be found on pages 26-33.

Furthermore, DIN SPEC 3105 outlines a process to verify the documentation criteria mentioned. This process is called the review process.

### CAB 2021

In the first round, we reviewed 15 open-source hardware documentations. Without going into detail about the specific projects, we would like to briefly explain what we learned about the review process.

1. Rebuilding in addition to the theoretical review

During the review of the MNT Reform open-source laptop, it became apparent that the project was so complex that it was very difficult for the reviewers to determine if all the information was available. A practical rebuild based on the documentation would have ensured this. For complex projects, a practical rebuild in addition to the theoretical review is helpful as it generates more valuable feedback.

2. Open-source hardware projects receive feedback

The documentation of an open-source hardware project benefits greatly from a critical and independent four-eye principle. Incomprehensible part descriptions are identified and avoided; sometimes, a picture is needed to make it clearer to rebuilders what it is about.

3. Community Building

The review process supports community building. Through the review, two people are brought closer to the project, requiring them to engage intensively with it.

4. Platform-independent reviewing

A project benefits from the analysis of the documentation even more if the reviewers use not only offline files or our platforms but also the project's platforms. They also test the documentation structure across different platforms and websites, putting themselves in the perspective of users who stumbled upon the project on the internet.

When a rebuild is connected with the review process, it can be ensured that the documentation is complete, and the project receives detailed feedback. This is exactly the goal of the process shown in this booklet. In follow-up projects of the association, open-source hardware projects will not only be theoretically reviewed but also practically rebuilt.

### Interview - From Idea to Reality

Synergy for a Better Future

Paul Anca and Ken Rostand are the co-founders of Open Funk. re:Mix went through the review process, whose process is presented on page 28. The following interview sheds light on the path towards open-source hardware from a startup perspective.

### What is re:Mix?

re:Mix is a sustainable kitchen mixer specifically designed for use with mason jars and is particularly durable. re:Mix is easily repairable, modular, and open source.

### What was your motivation to start an OSH startup?

From electronic waste and enormous CO₂ emissions to poor working conditions and long supply chains – the home electronics industry contributes to some of the world's most serious environmental and human rights problems of our time. We want to change the way household electronics are made and repaired. We see the circular economy as one of the key approaches to solving these problems.

Actors need to share information along the supply chain: What materials were used, where did they come from, and how can they be reused? At the same time, products must be designed for the circular economy from the start – repairable and modular.

Patents make this kind of collaboration impossible. They enrich a small group of people, but society benefits only minimally from the potential value of innovation. Open source, on the other hand, offers the opportunity to share information and improve and standardize it across industries.

### What challenges arise when founding an OSH startup?

When starting a startup, you get a lot of (unsolicited) advice. Before you even start implementing an idea, you are pressured to patent it. Unfortunately, many founders take this step without adequately considering the possible consequences. The biggest challenge for us was to find a unique selling proposition (USP) that is not based on the product itself (as it can theoretically be copied by others). In our case, we focus on high product quality and transparency around the product itself, as well as a local value chain and a "no-bullshit brand" on sustainability.

It is also important to consider how we can nurture the open-source community without losing focus on our core activities. We need to plan enough resources for this.

### What is your open-source strategy?

We have decided on a two-step strategy:

1. Housing including electronics, as a basis for mixers, coffee grinders, kitchen machines, citrus presses, etc. (published on 6.12.23)

2. Blender attachment - planned publication summer 2024

The housing was published first to promote repairs, upgrades, conversions, and discussions with others. The blender attachment ensures the safe use of the appliance when handling sharp blades and food and is to undergo further iterations before publication.

### What feedback do you receive from the OSH community?

So far, we have received very positive feedback from the open-source community. Several makerspaces, makers, and customers have contacted us because they want to build or modify the re:Mix themselves. The special thing about it is that a re:Mix built in Cameroon, for example, consists of local materials and thus has its own character.

### What motivation and benefits do you see in being part of the OSH community?

We see open source as a means to give people the right to repair: If you own a product, you should also have the right to repair it. At the same time, we create full transparency about what is in our products and spread repair-friendly designs among makers and industrial companies. The benefits we see include reducing negative impacts on the planet through decentralized production of repairable products; building a faster, better, and more inclusive product development process; moving people from consumers to prosumers - as informed, proactive shapers of society; and ultimately strengthening our brand by being transparent and open.

### Review & Rebuild

In the OSEG project "Validation & Rebuild of Circular Machines" (October 2022 to September 2023), three products were accompanied through the review process, with the documentation reviewed for completeness according to DIN SPEC 3105. The new aspect of this project was that a rebuild was conducted. This is an extension of the review with a practical component. Besides completeness, the documentation is checked for readability and comprehensibility.

Three products went through the process: the 3D printer Prusa i3+ MK3S, the kitchen mixer re:Mix, and the CNC mill MakersMill. To systematically capture questions and comments on the documentation, issues (tasks) were created in OSEG Gitlab. This way, the review and rebuild process is openly documented and can be reviewed and further developed in the future.

### Documentation

Why Documentation?

Only when the OSH product can be replicated and modified can collaboration truly happen. The documentation of the OSH project is therefore the central medium of collaboration. It should spark interest in the project and motivate people to get involved.

To build a community, communicate with it, and receive helpful contributions, good open documentation is indispensable. Writing documentation itself brings another benefit for the internal project team. Formulating thoughts and justifying decisions leads to questioning oneself and taking another perspective.

There are many types of documentation. This section and DIN SPEC 3105 do not prescribe how documentation should be designed. Instead, it encourages active creation and viewing of the project's documentation as an independent and important part of the project.

### What Belongs in Documentation?

For a hardware project to be called "open source," the documentation must provide certain information so others can replicate, modify, and use the project commercially without legal concerns.

DIN SPEC 3105 outlines what is needed. (https://din.one/display/OSH/DIN+SPEC+3105+OSH). Here, we explain in more detail what is specifically noted in DIN SPEC 3105.

### License

If you have chosen a license, add it along with the license text to your documentation. Mention in your project description which parts of the project fall under this license. This shows others how they may use your project and provides legal certainty for them and you.

### ReadMe / Overview

A brief project overview is helpful but not mandatory. What is the project about, what parts does the documentation consist of, and how is it structured? Who is the author? When was the last major change or version? Under which license does this project fall?

### Assembly Instructions

The assembly instructions explain how to build the project, possibly install software, etc. There are no limits to creativity here. The instructions can be designed as a video, text, or collage.

### Bill of Materials (BOM)

The BOM is a list of all parts included in the project. It is important that different types of parts require different information.

- **Standard Parts**: Standard parts must be correctly named as they are precisely described. "M6 screw" is not as specific as the somewhat cryptic designation: "EN ISO 4762 M6×40". This clearly defines the head shape, length, and diameter. It is generally advisable to use as many standard parts as possible since they are usually easily obtainable.

 

- **Purchased Parts**: For purchased parts (like electric motors), it is crucial to provide the exact product name, manufacturer, and performance description. More information is better than less. For replication, it is important to find the exact motor quickly. When modifying, it is also interesting to know why this particular motor was chosen.

- **Unique Parts**: All parts specifically manufactured for the project are called "unique parts." They should also be listed in the BOM, preferably with a reference to the source files! (See the next section)

### Replication vs. Modification

With a detailed BOM and assembly instructions, the right to replicate is already secured. To understand what a documentation must include to make a project not only replicable but also modifiable, it helps to focus on the assembly instructions themselves. Even the best instructions will likely have some errors, and it would be great if the community could easily fix these.

### Source Files

If you upload your instructions as a PDF, people who find an error can only copy everything error-free and send you the PDF. This is cumbersome and does not allow modifications. If you also upload the files from which you created the PDF, others can open these files (provided they have the right program), modify them, and then create a new PDF. These files are called "source files." They save your community a lot of work and make it much easier for them to get started and collaborate. So, it is important to provide not only the final product but also all the source files.

### Export Files

Source files are typically program-dependent, and editing these files can be expensive due to license costs. Therefore, it is important to provide an export version in an easily accessible format in addition to the source files. The PDF format for instructions is convenient because it can be opened without expensive software. In the case of machine parts, the export format can be a technical drawing, a .step file for 3D printed parts, or a FreeCAD file because FreeCAD is freely accessible. The export format is not fixed, but you should keep in mind to make it as easy as possible for your readers to open and use the file.

### Software

Software/firmware should also be included in the documentation, with a reference to where it can be found.

### Program Recommendations

You are free to use any program to create your files. We recommend some open-source programs as they make it easier for your community to get started.

- 3D Modeling (CAD) → FreeCAD

- PCB Development / CB Layout → KiCAD

- Collaboration and Versioning → Git (GitLab, Github, etc.)

- Database with many open-source software tools for all applications in machine and software development: https://opentoolchain.org/tools/

### Feedback

It is not easy to enable others to replicate and modify your project. It is easy to forget details or assume things that are completely unclear to others. Therefore, feedback from external people is very important to ensure others understand your formulations.

### Interview - Open to New Ideas?

How Open Source Democratizes Research at Universities

Ingeborg Beckers is a professor of medical physics at the Berlin University of Applied Sciences (BHT). She also heads the Laboratory for Optics and Laser Technology and has already published open-source hardware (OSH) and software in two projects.

Sven Klinkow works in technology transfer at BHT, handling inventions and aiming to network the university's departments and laboratories to share resources. He supports the spread of OSH at the university.

### What excites you about open-source hardware, and what do you see behind the open-source movement?

Sven: Especially in academia, where the free exchange of knowledge and reproduc

ibility of research results are of central importance, OSH can offer great added value. Open source excites me because it can be an enormous enrichment from a university perspective – an enrichment in the form of collaboration, discussions, and joint development. The resulting dynamic can be much greater compared to simply publishing the finished product or a project report. I am firmly convinced that this is a crucial factor.

### What does it take for more projects to go the OSH route?

Inge: I think a best-practices guide would be very helpful. I believe not many have tried to publish OSH, and it is unnecessary to make the same mistakes repeatedly. It must be clear at what development stage the published work is because OSH always involves user investment.

### What aspects need to be reconsidered to make an OSH project successful?

Inge: Additional manpower is needed! Either you can divert resources from research, or you specifically hire someone for the project. It is essential to actively maintain the community. I find exchange on a common platform important so that projects are not worked on in isolation but rather continuous communication takes place.

Sven: This requires a rethink in planning and conception. It may be necessary to leave the "traditional" research design and align with "new," OSH-oriented approaches, including greater involvement of the community in the development process.

### Intellectual Property and Licenses

There are three types of intellectual property:

1. The trademark of the project or company

2. Things protected by copyright

3. New technical contexts and ideas of the project

### Trademark Protection / "Trademark"

The trademark is the most important protection for the product. It identifies the manufacturer. Even if others can use the knowledge and produce the same product, they may not use the same trademarks as long as it is registered!

These can be names, logos, graphics, colors, as well as packaging and product designs. These make the project and products unique and recognizable.

### Copyright

By default, all creative works are protected by copyright from the moment of creation. This includes all project documentation, images, and code. Only through the open-source license is it made freely available. An open-source license must be actively chosen to open the knowledge and allow the community to work with it.

### Technical Contexts and Ideas

Technical contexts and ideas are the product of innovation and the work of developers. You can try to protect them with a patent. The process of applying for and defending a patent is costly and time-consuming. A patent aims to protect the idea for the next 20 years, promising the inventors a development lead. Owning a patent to then release the knowledge is rarely worthwhile.

If you publish your plans instead, the invention becomes "state of the art" and can no longer be patented ("defensive publishing"). Otherwise, large companies could patent an OSH project, and the inventors themselves would no longer be allowed to work on it. To make this work, you need a resilient timestamp; this is why git-based version control systems are suitable. If the idea is already patented, there can still be patent disputes.

### Open-Source Hardware Licenses

Licensing the documentation and hardware clarifies under what conditions the knowledge can be reused. This legal certainty protects contributors and users. Only this way can a group be formed that promotes the development process and gives the project a wide and targeted reach.

For hardware, we recommend a variant of CERN OHL v2.0. It is explicitly written for hardware, continuously maintained, and comes in three variants like many open-source licenses.

### Permissive

Permissive licenses give people the most freedom in reusing the project. Usually, only the author must be mentioned. Here are some examples:

- MIT License

- CERN-OHL-P-2.0

- Creative Commons International 4.0 - CC BY 4.0

### Copyleft

Copyleft licenses require contributors to release all new versions of the project under a copyleft license. There are "strong" and "weak" copyleft licenses.

- "Strong" copyleft licenses require everything related to the project to be released under the same license, while "weak" copyleft licenses only require the project itself to be released under the same open-source license.

Examples of copyleft licenses:

- GNU General Public License Version 3.0 – GPL v3 (for software)

- CERN Open Hardware License Version 2 – Strongly Reciprocal – CERN-OHL-S-2.0

- GNU Lesser General Public License Version 3 – LGPL v3 (for software)

- CERN Open Hardware License Version 2 – Weakly Reciprocal – CERN-OHL-W-2.0

- Creative Commons Share Alike International 4.0 – CC BY-SA 4.0 (universally applicable, images, texts, videos, etc.)

More information on this topic can be found in these sources:

- https://choosealicense.com/

- Chapter "Licensing of Open Source Hardware" by Michael Weinberg from the book "Open Source Hardware" by Alicia Gibb and others

- https://spdx.org/licenses/

### Open-Source Business Models and Ecosystems

Open-Source Hardware: From Product Development to Business Model to Ecosystem

Without open-source software, the internet and many digital innovations would not be conceivable. In times when urgently needed global solutions must be found, open-source hardware (OSH) seems to play a similarly important role in the physical world. However, it appears that there is a bottleneck between product development and market entry. What could be some reasons for this?

For open-source software, the transition from development to deployment was relatively easy because the distribution of code is almost cost-neutral, without time delay, and globally possible. Support, service, SaaS, and add-ons have also established themselves as monetization options. These are so successful that some open-source startups focus on proprietary parts and become more closed.

With OSH, the challenges are more complex due to the physical nature of the product, and new technical processes make it easier for competitors not involved in development. Nevertheless, one can assume that startups based on open hardware can be competitive with proprietary technical systems.

From idea to market entry, several phases are passed through, each requiring different skills, processes, and activities. Initially, the community for a project must be built to gain partners for development. Then, it should be clarified whether the product will find a market and what organizational form is suitable.

For proprietary technical startups, there are relatively well-defined procedures and the recommendation to cover the various areas through a founding team. Securing rights is considered an important prerequisite for competitiveness.

For OSH, however, openness is the underlying principle to achieve collective innovations through often decentralized collaboration and knowledge sharing. Different license forms can determine the usage.

Openness, collaboration, and decentralization allow for great dynamics in innovation processes but lead to higher complexity and thus more need for communication and coordination.

This can be an advantage, especially in the early phase, and can be compensated by access to community resources. In addition to monetary values, social and ecological values are also created:

The community is the first customer in terms of participation in the project, and possible user groups can then be quickly identified through them. The value must already be visible to both at this point.

Development can advance in the safe harbor of the community without agreeing on a business model at an early stage. Risks and costs are reduced, quality and reliability are increased, and changes are possible even in later phases.

Using existing open-source solutions as a contribution to one's own idea accelerates the development process and reduces the risk of violating unknown patents, considering various OSH licenses.

In addition to monetary values, the open-source idea also includes social and ecological values that have not yet been explicitly given a place in classic business models. They are primarily recorded as cost factors, not as fundamental values. As such, they must be integrated into the OSH business model, allowing for the development of entirely new forms of organizations. Instead of the classic business model, ecosystems are created that are interconnected.

The new framework changes the perspective, making niches for inventions, improvements, and new actors visible. OSH entrepreneurship becomes a real possibility to solve pressing problems collectively and earn a living with meaningful work. Possibly in entirely new forms of collaboration during development and new ways of utilization that are yet to be defined. This creates more opportunities for accelerated, environmentally friendly, and socially accepted innovations and sustainable economies.

The open-source hardware idea resonated particularly well in Germany, leading to the formation of a broad community. This community founded the association OSEG e.V. to solidify and support the movement.

The association covers various areas, including concrete hardware projects, global community building, and networking. It is also active in public relations and political work, serving as a contact

 point and initiator. The association's community has contributed to developing initial standards and publications. A focus is on the circular economy, common good economy, and sustainability, which are anchored in the association's activities. For OSEG e.V., it is important that the community can live through OSH.
